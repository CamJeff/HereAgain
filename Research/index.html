Microwave-Based Imaging for Bladder State Detection

Supervised by Prof. Emily Porter.

  • The entire lab is centered around developing microwave-based medical technologies (both for imaging, and for treatment techniques such as ablation). We like microwave radiation for imaging since it's non-ionizing, very inexpensive, easy to use, and comfortable for the patient.
  • The project I worked on involved developing a wearable path antenna array that is able to detect the state of the bladder (is it full or empty). The idea for this was spurred from a charity approaching our lab that deals with children with ASD/IDD that may experience urinary incontinence. The aim of the antenna device is to let users know when their bladder is full and they have to use the bathroom (or 'void').
  • Started with one wearable antenna and optimized the dimensions and geometry by assessing the S11 parameter loss.
  • This then slowly built to an antenna array with more complex and realistic computational 3D phantoms of the pelvis, derived from CT scans of real patients.
  • Also investigated other non-wearable antennas such as a Vivaldi design, which is said to be more directive, and thus can resolve the signal difference more clearly between full and empty bladders.
  • Read out most recent paper submission to learn more here.
Dates March 2024 – Present

Dose Surface Mapping of the Parotid Glands

Supervised by Prof. John Kildea.

  • This a project I recently started for my undergraduate thesis. The motivation comes from the fact that patients who undergo offline adaptive radiation therapy to treat cancer of the head and neck can often experience side effects. One of which is a loss of function of parotid glands, the structure responsible for producing saliva, in a condition known as xerostomia or dry mouth.
  • We want to develop a predictive dose surface mapping software, in that we can visualize exactly where the radiation dose goes in the parotid gland during treatment. The dose surface maps is meant to produce a 2D image over the structure of interest after it has been “flattened out”.
  • For the parotid glands, we aim to look at dose surface maps for concentric spherical shells (like an onion) after they have flattened (distortion is inevitable, but we can also quarter the parotids as well).
  • Work on this project is still in early stages, but now I am testing on very simple spherical computational phantoms that I made myself (with fake dose information also given by me). We will soon move to more anatomically accurate models.
Dates Sept. 2025 – Present
