February 2026: Subtiles 2

Puzzle Statement

Place positive integers in some of the cells above, so that the grid contains one $1$, two $2$’s, three $3$’s, and so on, up to $N$ $N$’s. (For some $N$.)

For any integer $K$ in the grid, the cells labeled $K$ must form an orthogonally connected region (a “$K$-omino”). Furthermore, for each $K > 1$ the $K$-omino must “contain” the shape formed by the ($K-1$)’s. (Rotations and reflections are allowed.)

Some of the cells have been labeled, but unlike our previous Subtiles, we have used variables to obscure those values. (As in the example, at the bottom.)

After completing the grid, compute, in each row, the sum of the labeled cells. The answer to this puzzle is the product of the maximum and minimum row sums.

Solution

A very fun grid puzzle that feels unique in that it's not as daunting as it looks. I find some of the grid puzzles from Jane Street look so horrendous that the only way to solve it in a somewhat reasonable time is to design a program to solve it for you. Solving it this way takes a lot of the fun out of these puzzles in my opinion, but sometimes it seems there is no choice. I strongly believe that these grid puzzles should NOT be designed that way, that is, anybody could sit down and solve it by hand with some effort.

I appreciated this one because it's very doable to solve by hand, and in fact, is probably much faster than writing a program to solve it automatically. The first thing to do is to determine the values of $a$, $b$, and $c$. I wasn't very methodical with this, but knowing each expression is an integer between $1$ and maximum $17$, I just guessed and checked some numbers. It became clear that $a=\frac 14$, $b=-3$, and $c=\frac 12$.

Once that was done, I decided to quickly make an interactive version of the puzzle to ease the process of solving. Check out the tool HERE. To use it, simply click, hold, and drag from a numbered cell to neighboring empty cells to fill them. To delete numbered cell, click on it.
